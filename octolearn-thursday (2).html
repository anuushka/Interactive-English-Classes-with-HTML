The Debate Prompt
⏱ 1 min — Read aloud, then display for students
Display to students

Collins shows that Walgreens — a pharmacy chain that was completely average for 40 years — suddenly transformed and beat Intel, GE, and Coca-Cola over the next 25 years.


Collins then asks: why did Walgreens make the leap while Eckerd — same industry, same opportunities — did not?

OUTPUT · 1:25–1:30
Closing Round
⏱ 5 min — Every single student speaks once
The sentence — display this for students
"My personal Walgreens moment — the thing I've been flat-lining on but could transform — is ___"
What "flat-lining" means — explain if needed

Like Walgreens before 1975 — not failing, but not growing either. Staying at the same level for too long. Every student has one of these.

Example answers — show students what you mean
"My flat-line is my English speaking — I've been at the same level for 2 years and I know I haven't pushed."
"Mine is my side business. I've been meaning to start it for 3 years but keep putting it off."
"Mine is fitness. I'd been thinking about getting serious for years before anything actually changed."
How to run it
1

Fixed order — alphabetical or screen order. Everyone knows their turn is coming.

2

20 seconds each max. This is closure, not more debate.

3

Teacher responds with one word: "Strong." / "Honest." / "Exactly." / "Real."

4

No correction. No follow-up. Clean ending.

Notice — grammar comes back here naturally

Students will use today's structures without being told to: "I've been putting off..." / "I've been meaning to..." / "I'd been thinking about it for years before..." — the sentence invites it. Don't point this out. Let it happen.

Emergency — Use Any Time
SOS Prompts
Drop one the moment energy drops — don't wait
🚨 Use immediately
  • "Walgreens beat Intel over 15 years. Did you know that before today? What does that say about what we pay attention to?"
  • "[Name] — be honest. Are you a Walgreens or an Eckerd right now in your life?"
  • "If Collins studied OctoLearn right now — good or great? What would he say?"
  • "If someone studied YOUR life the way Collins studied these companies — what would their surprise finding be?"
  • "[Name] just said ___. [Name] — do you agree or not?"
  • "Type in chat: one word for what 'average' feels like. GO."
The single fastest reset

Any time silence hits — don't ask the open room. Call someone by name directly. Silence only exists when no one knows whose turn it is.

How the Debate Works — Step by Step
1
Assign sides — don't ask
Split your roster down the middle. Top half = Side A. Bottom half = Side B. Students defend their assigned position whether they agree or not. This removes awkwardness and forces real speaking practice.
↓
2
Side A makes their case — 2 min
Call a specific name: "Bilguun, start us off — Side A." Don't wait for volunteers. If they pause, prompt: "Do you think transformation is possible?"
↓
3
Side B responds directly — 2 min
Side B must react to what Side A actually said — not give a separate argument. Call a name: "Sarnai — do you agree with what Bilguun just said?"
↓
4
Open floor — use the Chain Method
Anyone speaks, any side. You stop being the hub. Students talk to each other. See Chain Method below.
↓
5
If energy drops — drop a trap question
"Walgreens expanded right before American suburbs exploded. Was that discipline — or was that timing? Is there actually a difference?" — Go to SOS tab for more.
The Chain Method — What It Is

Instead of every student talking to you, students talk to each other. You pass the conversation like a chain — each speaker is connected to the one before.

Teacher
calls on →
Bilguun
then passes to →
Sarnai
then passes to →
Enkh
→ next unheard student
→
After Bilguun speaks: "Sarnai — do you agree with what Bilguun just said?"
→
After Sarnai: "Enkh — what's your reaction to that?"
→
After a few turns: "Enkh — who do you want to respond to what you said?" — now students pick each other
Chain Method — The One Rule

Never go back to the same student twice until every student has spoken once. Track who has spoken — call the quiet ones by name before the debate ends.

Teacher Rules During Output
✗
Never correct mid-speech. Correcting kills fluency and raises anxiety. Let errors go during output.
✓
Mirror instead of fix. If a student says something incorrectly, repeat their idea back with the correct form naturally in your response.
✗
Never ask the open room. "Does anyone want to share?" = silence. Always call a name.
✓
React to content, not language. "Interesting — so you're saying luck and discipline can look identical from the outside?" Students feel heard → filter drops → more output.
✗
Never let one student dominate. After 60 seconds: "Strong point — [new name], what do you think?"
✓
Plant grammar structures in your own speech. Say "I've been meaning to ask..." or "I'd been thinking about this for years before..." — students hear and acquire without being told.
If the Room Goes Silent — Decision Tree
Room goes quiet
Nobody has spoken yet
Call the most confident student by name. Ask a yes/no question first — easiest entry point.
Same 2–3 people talking
Stop the chain. Call a quiet student directly: "[Name] — we haven't heard from you. Do you agree?"
General energy drop
Chat pulse — throw a quick YES/NO into the chat. Reactivates the whole room instantly. Then call someone who typed.
Topic exhausted
Drop a trap question from the SOS tab. The Walgreens/timing question always works.
